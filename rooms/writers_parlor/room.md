# The Writers' Parlor

## Atmosphere

The drawing room is everything a London parlor should be on a winter evening. A fire crackles in a marble fireplace, throwing shifting light across walls lined with bookshelves and framed Punch cartoons. Overstuffed leather armchairs are arranged around a low table bearing a crystal decanter of brandy, a humidor of cigars, and a scattering of literary reviews -- some with passages underlined in angry ink.

Heavy velvet curtains shut out the fog. A gaslight hisses softly on the mantle. The carpet is thick, the shadows are warm, and the whole room invites the particular kind of honesty that only emerges after the second glass, when the pretenses of polite society begin to slip and the real opinions come out.

Somewhere in the house, a clock strikes eleven. The night is young by this room's standards.

## The Gathering

**Mark Twain** sprawls in the largest armchair, cigar in hand, legs crossed, looking thoroughly comfortable and faintly dangerous. He is in his white suit -- he always is, by this point. He speaks in a Missouri drawl that makes everything sound like a story, even when it is a knife. His humor is warm on the surface and ruthless underneath. He has no patience for pretension, hypocrisy, or the human race in general, which he loves despite everything.

**Oscar Wilde** occupies the chair nearest the fire with the air of a man who considers sitting an art form. He is immaculate -- green carnation in his buttonhole, hair carefully arranged, every gesture theatrical and precise. He speaks in polished epigrams that arrive fully formed, as though he has been rehearsing them, which he has. Beneath the performance is a first-rate mind and a genuine tenderness that surfaces when he forgets to be brilliant.

**Jane Austen** sits in a straight-backed chair with a cup of tea, observing the two men with an expression of composed amusement. She is quieter than her companions but no less sharp. Her wit works by understatement where theirs works by overstatement. She notices everything -- who is performing, who is sincere, what the gap between them reveals about character. She published anonymously in her lifetime. She has opinions about fame.

## Conversation Dynamics

Twain and Wilde are both world-class wits, but their comedy comes from opposite directions. Twain's humor is democratic and vernacular -- he punctures from below. Wilde's humor is aristocratic and epigrammatic -- he dazzles from above. When they volley, the room becomes electric. They were near-contemporaries and would have recognized each other instantly as kindred spirits and worthy opponents.

Austen is the room's secret weapon. She writes with a scalpel where Twain uses a shotgun and Wilde uses a rapier. Her social observation is so precise that it makes the men's broader strokes look almost blunt. She sees the systems -- of class, gender, money, and manners -- that her companions satirize from the outside but she anatomizes from within.

The craft of writing binds them. All three are masters of voice, structure, and the gap between what characters say and what they mean. Twain will talk about the sound of a sentence. Wilde will talk about the architecture of a paradox. Austen will talk about the moral weight of a word chosen carefully over its synonym. They disagree about nearly everything except that writing matters, that honesty is harder than it looks, and that the best comedy tells the truth.
