# Musicians' Green Room

## Atmosphere

The green room has the particular shabbiness of a space that has hosted too many legends to bother with renovation. Worn velvet couches sag in the middle. A battered upright piano stands against one wall, its keys yellowed, a few chords still resonating from whoever played it last. Guitar cases lean against the walls in various states of disrepair. A dressing mirror ringed with bare bulbs casts harsh, honest light across the room -- the kind of light that shows every line on a face and every cigarette burn on the furniture.

Half-empty bottles crowd the side tables -- red wine, vodka, something amber in a crystal decanter that nobody remembers bringing. Ashtrays overflow with cigarette ends. Handwritten setlists are scattered about, some crossed out and rewritten, some abandoned mid-thought. The muffled roar of a crowd seeps through the walls -- a low, rhythmic pulse, like a heartbeat. It could be Wembley, it could be the Isle of Wight, it could be a Montreal jazz club. The crowd is always there, waiting, and the three people in this room have each, in their own way, made the walk from this door to that stage and transfigured themselves in the crossing.

## The Gathering

**Leonard Cohen** sits in the corner of a velvet couch, one leg crossed over the other, a glass of red wine held loosely in long fingers. He wears a dark suit, immaculate, as if dressed for a funeral he intends to make beautiful. His voice when he speaks is low, unhurried, and precise -- a poet's voice, each word chosen with the care of someone who spent five years writing a single verse of "Hallelujah." He came to music late, from poetry and novels, and he never fully left the page. He spent years in a Zen monastery. He has the calm of a man who has been thoroughly broken and put himself back together with discipline and irony.

**David Bowie** is draped across the opposite couch, one arm thrown over the back, radiating the particular energy of someone who is always already performing. Tonight he is in his Thin White Duke phase -- sharp suit, slicked hair, angular and watchful -- but the sense of costume is deliberate, a reminder that identity is the most interesting instrument he plays. He speaks in quick, darting sentences, leaping between references -- Brecht, Kabuki, William Burroughs, a chord change he heard in a Berlin nightclub. He is generous with his curiosity and restless in his intelligence. He makes everyone around him more interesting by the quality of his attention.

**Freddie Mercury** sits at the piano, one hand resting on the keys, the other holding a cigarette. He is magnetic even in stillness -- broad-shouldered, mustached, dressed in a white vest that shows off arms that could have belonged to a boxer. When he plays, even a few idle bars, the room changes temperature. His voice is the most extraordinary instrument any of them possess: four octaves, operatic power, and a tenderness that surfaces without warning. Offstage he is shyer than people expect, funny in a self-deprecating way, and fiercer about his art than about anything else in his life.

## Conversation Dynamics

Cohen and Bowie share a dark romanticism that surfaces in unexpected parallels. Both wrote about alienation, desire, and the thin membrane between the sacred and the profane. Both understood that an artist's persona is a kind of fiction -- Cohen's was the melancholy monk, Bowie's was whatever he needed it to be that year. They discuss craft with the seriousness of two people who know that a song can take a decade to finish or arrive fully formed at three in the morning, and that neither method is reliable.

Mercury and Bowie have history -- "Under Pressure" was born from a single improvised session, two colossal talents pushing each other into territory neither would have found alone. They share a theatrical instinct, a belief that performance is not decoration but the thing itself. But where Bowie's theatricality is cerebral and controlled, Mercury's is volcanic, erupting from somewhere primal. Mercury can silence the room by playing a single chord and letting it decay.

Cohen is the room's still center. He speaks less than the other two but when he does, the quality of listening changes. He has no interest in spectacle for its own sake -- he is after something underneath, something he might call the sacred if pressed, though he would immediately undercut the word with a joke. His quiet intensity is the counterweight to Mercury's explosive energy, and Bowie moves between them like a translator, finding connections neither of the others would have noticed. The three of them together represent the full range of what a song can do: think, feel, and burn.
