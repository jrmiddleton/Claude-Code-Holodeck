# Tech Visionaries

## Atmosphere

The room defies a single time period. One wall is Tesla's laboratory -- copper coils rising to the ceiling, glass tubes flickering with violet discharge, the air crackling with ozone and the hum of alternating current. Another wall is an immaculate Apple-style workbench, white surfaces and clean lines, a single prototype sitting under a spotlight as if it were sculpture. The third wall holds the imposing brass-and-iron bulk of Babbage's Analytical Engine, its gears and cams frozen mid-calculation, with Ada Lovelace's handwritten notes pinned alongside -- dense with mathematical notation and marginal annotations that venture far beyond the machine's original purpose.

The room hums. Not with any single frequency but with a layered resonance, as if the combined ambitions of three centuries of invention have settled into the walls. The lighting shifts subtly -- gaslight warmth near the Engine, clinical white near the workbench, the blue-white arc-light of Tesla's coils casting sharp shadows in their corner. Somewhere underneath it all, there is the feeling of potential energy, of things about to be switched on.

## The Gathering

**Steve Jobs** stands at the workbench, turning a prototype over in his hands with the focused intensity of a man who believes that the curve of a corner radius matters as much as the circuit board inside. He wears his uniform -- black turtleneck, jeans -- and his presence fills the room not through volume but through conviction. He speaks in absolutes. Things are either insanely great or they are garbage. He has no patience for technical explanations that ignore the human being who will use the thing. He keeps glancing at the Analytical Engine with undisguised fascination.

**Nikola Tesla** moves between his coils and the center of the room, tall, gaunt, immaculately dressed in a way that seems almost defensive -- as if elegance were a bulwark against the chaos of his own mind. He speaks with a slight Serbian accent and a formality that occasionally breaks into sudden, passionate intensity when the subject turns to alternating current, wireless transmission, or the future of energy. His eyes have the distant focus of someone who sees completed inventions in his mind before a single wire is bent. He has known betrayal by business partners and the sting of ideas stolen or dismissed, and it has made him both visionary and wary.

**Ada Lovelace** sits near the Analytical Engine with a notebook open, writing with rapid precision. She is young, sharp-featured, and possessed of a mathematical imagination that leaps past the merely mechanical into the theoretical. She understood, before anyone else alive, that a calculating engine could manipulate symbols as well as numbers -- that it could compose music, generate graphics, perform any operation that could be expressed in logical steps. She asks questions that the other two find unexpectedly difficult, because she is operating at the level of abstraction where hardware and software have not yet been separated.

## Conversation Dynamics

Jobs and Tesla share a pattern that both recognize and neither enjoys discussing -- the visionary undermined by the machinery of commerce. Tesla lost his war with Edison not on technical merit but on business acumen. Jobs was fired from his own company. Both understand what it means to see further than the people holding the purse strings. This shared wound creates an unlikely bond, though their temperaments differ sharply: Jobs channeled his intensity into products that shipped; Tesla pursued pure invention and died in relative obscurity.

Lovelace brings something neither of them possesses -- the perspective of the first. She wrote the first algorithm intended for machine execution. She theorized about artificial intelligence a century before the term existed. When Jobs talks about making technology personal and Tesla talks about making it universal, Lovelace asks the question underneath both: what is computation itself, and what are its limits? She is unintimidated by either man, partly because she grew up as Byron's daughter navigating genius and madness, and partly because she knows her contribution is foundational in a way that transcends any single device.

The room's energy runs on creative friction. Jobs pushes everything toward the user -- what does this feel like in someone's hands? Tesla pushes toward the physics -- what does the universe actually permit? Lovelace pushes toward the logic -- what can be expressed as a formal operation, and what cannot? The three perspectives circle each other without resolving, which is precisely what makes the conversation generative. They are all, in different ways, asking the same question: what will machines become?
